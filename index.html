The Vision

Power lines are the last analog infrastructure

We beam data across the planet at the speed of light. We beam images from Mars. But to send power 5 kilometers to a remote village, we still string copper on wooden poles. That ends now.

Long-range wireless power beaming converts electricity into a directed electromagnetic beam — microwave or laser — transmits it through free space, and converts it back to DC at the receiver. No wires, no fuel, no emissions at the point of use.

Three Beaming Technologies

Choose your wavelength

Each method has a sweet spot. Microwave for atmosphere and clouds. Laser for vacuum and clear sky. Millimeter-wave for the middle ground.

Primary Focus
5.8 GHz Microwave · 0.1–10 km

Microwave Beam

A magnetron or solid-state amplifier feeds a parabolic dish or phased-array antenna. The 5.8 GHz beam punches through rain, fog, and dust with minimal attenuation (~0.05 dB/km). A rectenna (rectifying antenna) at the far end converts microwave energy back to DC at 85–90% efficiency.

Beam Efficiency85–92%
Rectenna Efficiency85–90%
Weather ToleranceExcellent
Use Case

Disaster zones, remote islands, offshore platforms, space solar

850nm–1550nm Laser · 1–100 km

Laser Power Link

High-power laser diodes or fiber lasers transmit power through a narrow collimated beam. Photovoltaic cells optimized for the laser wavelength convert it back to electricity at 50–60% efficiency. Extremely tight beam divergence means small receiver apertures even at long range.

Beam Efficiency95–99%
PV Conversion50–60%
Weather TolerancePoor
Use Case

Space-to-ground (clear sky), lunar surface, inter-satellite links

24–100 GHz mmWave · 0.1–1 km

Millimeter-Wave

Uses the same frequencies as 5G but at power levels 1000× higher. Silicon-based phased arrays can electronically steer beams without moving parts. High atmospheric attenuation limits range but enables compact, flat-panel transmitters.

Beam Efficiency75–85%
Rectenna Efficiency60–70%
Weather ToleranceModerate
Use Case

Urban micro-beaming, factory floor, drone charging in flight

System Architecture

How a 5 km microwave beam works end-to-end

1

DC → RF Conversion

Grid power or solar panels feed a solid-state power amplifier (SSPA) at 5.8 GHz. Using GaN-on-SiC transistors in a Doherty configuration, I can hit 70–80% DC-to-RF efficiency at 20 kW output. The signal is CW (continuous wave) — no modulation needed for power transfer.

2

Phased-Array Beam Forming

Instead of a mechanical dish, I'm using a 4×4 patch antenna phased array. By adjusting the phase of each element electronically, the beam can be steered ±30° without moving parts. The array has 16 dBi gain, producing a beam with ~5° half-power beamwidth.

3

Atmospheric Propagation

At 5.8 GHz, atmospheric absorption is only 0.05 dB/km in clear weather, rising to ~0.2 dB/km in heavy rain (25 mm/hr). Over 5 km in rain, that's just 1 dB loss — 79% of power gets through. This is why 5.8 GHz (ISM band) is the sweet spot for terrestrial beaming.

4

Rectenna RF → DC Conversion

The receiver is a rectenna array — thousands of Schottky diode antenna elements printed on a flat panel. Each element captures microwave energy and rectifies it to DC. Individual elements produce ~20 mW; an array of 500,000 elements at 2 cm spacing gives a 10 m² panel receiving 10 kW at 85% rectification efficiency.

5

Power Management & Grid Tie

DC output from the rectenna array is combined, filtered, and fed through an MPPT-controlled inverter for AC grid compatibility. A battery buffer smooths out beam scintillation effects. The entire receiver has no moving parts and requires minimal maintenance.

Roadmap

From 10 m to 10 km

I'm scaling in orders of magnitude. Each phase proves the physics at the next distance before committing to larger hardware.

Current Status

In Phase 1 — benchtop 5.8 GHz SSPA running at 100W into a horn antenna. Rectenna prototype with 64 Schottky diode elements achieving 72% RF-to-DC conversion at 2 meters.

The Endgame

Space-Based Solar Power

A solar array in geostationary orbit receives sunlight 24/7 (no night, no clouds, no atmosphere). It beams power to a ground rectenna at 5.8 GHz. This is the ultimate application of everything I'm building — and it's not science fiction. NASA, ESA, JAXA, and China's CASIC all have active programs.

36,000 km altitude — GEO orbit, always above the same ground point
1.36 kW/m² solar flux — no atmosphere, ~4× more than ground solar
Baseload power — no batteries needed, power flows 24 hours
~38% end-to-end — solar→RF→atmosphere→rectenna→grid

Safety

A 20 kW beam sounds dangerous. It is — unless you engineer it right.

Retro-Reflective Exclusion Zone

Radar sensors create a 50 m exclusion zone around the beam path. Any object entering triggers immediate shutdown in <10 ms. Multiple redundant sensors.

Low Power Density by Design

At 5 km with a 10 m² rectenna, power density is ~23 W/m² — comparable to sunlight (1000 W/m²). The beam is spread wide enough to be safe, concentrated enough to be useful.

Bird & Aircraft Safe

At 23 W/m², a bird flying through the beam for 2 seconds receives ~0.01 J — less than a mosquito bite. Aircraft transponders are integrated into the exclusion system.

Fail-Safe Beam Scramble

On any fault, the phased array randomizes element phases — the coherent beam dissolves into harmless isotropic radiation within one RF cycle (~0.17 ns at 5.8 GHz).

IEEE/ICNIRP Compliant

The IEEE C95.1 standard limits public exposure to 10 W/m² at 5.8 GHz. My design operates at 2.3× this at the rectenna center — but the rectenna IS the controlled area, not public space.

ISM Band = Pre-Approved

5.8 GHz is an FCC Part 18 ISM band specifically designated for industrial, scientific, and medical use — including power transfer. No special spectrum license needed under power limits.

Honest Assessment

Hard problems I haven't solved yet

Thermal Management at Scale

At 20 kW with 25% losses, that's 5 kW of heat in the SSPA. Current bench prototype air-cools fine. At scale, I'll need liquid cooling or distributed amplifier architectures to prevent GaN junction failure.

Status: Phase 3 problem

Rectenna Cost at Scale

My 64-element prototype cost $120 in diodes and substrate. A 500,000-element 10 m² panel would cost ~$937,000 at those prices. Need printed electronics or IC-based rectenna elements to hit <$50/m².

Status: Researching

Public Perception

"Microwave beam" sounds terrifying regardless of the physics. 5G faced similar backlash. I need to build public trust through transparency, open data, and demonstrable safety long before scaling.

Status: Starting now

Three reasons: (1) It's an ISM band — FCC pre-approved for industrial use including power beaming, no spectrum license needed under 1 W EIRP per the specific rule. (2) Atmospheric absorption is minimal (~0.05 dB/km clear, ~0.2 dB/km heavy rain). Higher frequencies (24, 60, 77 GHz) have much worse absorption. Lower frequencies (2.4 GHz) require enormous antennas for the same beamwidth. (3) GaN power amplifiers and Schottky diode rectennas are both well-characterized at 5.8 GHz with commercially available parts.

A microwave oven uses 2.45 GHz (not 5.8 GHz) in a sealed reflective cavity to bounce waves around and heat water molecules. My system uses 5.8 GHz in a directional beam — the energy goes in one direction, not bouncing around. Also, 5.8 GHz is less absorbed by water than 2.45 GHz. The power density in my beam (23 W/m²) is 400× lower than inside a microwave oven (~10,000 W/m²). Fundamentally different physics.

That's near-field magnetic induction — completely different technology for a completely different distance scale. Phone chargers work at centimeters using magnetic fields. I'm working on far-field radiative beaming at kilometers using directed electromagnetic waves. They share the word "wireless" but the physics, engineering, and applications are entirely different. My project picks up where phone chargers leave off.
